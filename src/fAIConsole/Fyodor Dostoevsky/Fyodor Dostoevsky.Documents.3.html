Crime and Punishment

"Crime and Punishment" by Fyodor Dostoevsky is a psychological novel set in 19th-century Russia. The protagonist, Rodion Raskolnikov, is a poor ex-student who, in an attempt to escape his miserable condition, murders a pawnbroker for her money. He believes that he is justified in this act, considering himself an extraordinary person who is not bound by moral laws. However, he is consumed by guilt and paranoia after the crime. The novel explores his mental anguish and moral dilemmas. He eventually confesses to the crime and is sentenced to Siberian exile, where he seeks redemption and spiritual rebirth through suffering and the love of a woman named Sonia.

The Brothers Karamazov

"The Brothers Karamazov" is a profound philosophical and psychological novel by Fyodor Dostoevsky. It revolves around the complex, conflicting dynamics of the Karamazov brothers - Dmitri, Ivan, and Alyosha - and their morally corrupt father, Fyodor. The narrative explores themes of faith, doubt, free will, and morality, set against the backdrop of Russian society. The plot thickens when Fyodor is murdered, and Dmitri is accused. Ivan grapples with existential questions, while Alyosha, a novice monk, embodies faith and virtue. The novel is a deep exploration of the human soul, the struggle between good and evil, and the search for God.

The Idiot

"The Idiot" by Fyodor Dostoevsky is a profound exploration of the human condition. The protagonist, Prince Myshkin, is a nobleman with a pure heart and an innocent demeanor, often perceived as an 'idiot' by society. He returns to Russia from a Swiss sanatorium and becomes entangled in a complex love triangle with the beautiful but tormented Nastasya Filippovna and the virtuous Aglaya. His naivety and compassion lead to tragic consequences, as he is manipulated by those around him. The novel delves into themes of morality, society, and the struggle between good and evil, ultimately questioning what it means to be an 'idiot' in a corrupt world.

Notes from Underground

"Notes from Underground" by Fyodor Dostoevsky is a profound psychological exploration of an isolated, anonymous narrator, known as the Underground Man. The book is divided into two parts. In the first part, the Underground Man, a bitter and misanthropic retired civil servant living in St. Petersburg, critiques contemporary social and philosophical beliefs, particularly the concept of utopianism. In the second part, he recounts episodes from his past, revealing his struggles with social alienation, his cruel treatment of others, and his inability to form meaningful relationships. The book is a deep dive into the human psyche, exploring themes of free will, existentialism, and the nature of human happiness.

The Possessed

"The Possessed," also known as "Demons," is a complex novel by Fyodor Dostoevsky that explores the political and social chaos in 19th-century Russia. The story revolves around a group of revolutionaries, led by the charismatic but manipulative Pyotr Verkhovensky, who aim to overthrow the existing social order. Their plans, however, lead to disastrous consequences, including murder and suicide. The novel delves into the psychological depths of its characters, revealing their inner turmoil and moral dilemmas. Dostoevsky uses this narrative to critique radical ideologies and to highlight the destructive potential of nihilism and anarchism. The book is a profound exploration of human nature, societal structures, and the consequences of extreme ideologies.

Poor Folk

"Poor Folk" is Fyodor Dostoevsky's first novel, which presents a poignant tale of two distant relatives, Makar Devushkin and Varvara Dobroselova, living in abject poverty in St. Petersburg. The story unfolds through their letters, revealing their struggles, hopes, and fears. Devushkin, a low-paid government clerk, and Dobroselova, a seamstress, share a deep affection, but their impoverished circumstances prevent them from marrying. The novel explores themes of poverty, societal status, and unrequited love. Dostoevsky masterfully portrays the psychological impact of poverty and the tragic irony of love that cannot be fulfilled due to societal constraints.

The Eternal Husband

"The Eternal Husband" by Fyodor Dostoevsky is a psychological novella that explores the complex relationship between two men, Velchaninov and Trusotsky, who are connected through a deceased woman, Natalia, who was Trusotsky's wife and Velchaninov's former lover. The story unfolds as Trusotsky confronts Velchaninov about their shared past, leading to a tense and uncomfortable friendship. The narrative delves into themes of guilt, remorse, and the haunting past. Trusotsky's denial of his wife's infidelity and Velchaninov's guilt over the affair form the crux of the story. The climax reveals that Natalia's daughter might be Velchaninov's child, adding another layer of complexity to their relationship.

The House of the Dead

"The House of the Dead" by Fyodor Dostoevsky is a semi-autobiographical novel that explores life in a Siberian prison camp. The protagonist, Aleksandr Petrovich Goryanchikov, is a nobleman sentenced to penal servitude for murdering his wife. The narrative vividly portrays the harsh conditions, brutal punishments, and dehumanizing effects of the prison system. Despite the grim setting, Dostoevsky also highlights the prisoners' resilience, camaraderie, and moments of humanity. The novel is a profound exploration of human nature, suffering, and redemption, and a critique of the Russian penal system.
